Project Proposal: Paraphrase
Detection and Semantic Textual
Similarity Using Deep Learning
Natural Language Processing (NLP) systems are increasingly focused on understanding the
semantic meaning of text beyond its surface form. Two closely related NLP tasks, Paraphrase
Detection (PD) and Semantic Textual Similarity (STS), aim to quantify the similarity between two
texts in terms of meaning irrespective of diﬀerences in wording or syntax. Paraphrase detection is
typically a binary classification problem where the goal is to determine if two sentences convey
the same meaning. Semantic textual similarity, on the other hand, is modelled as a regression or
ranking task assigning a graded similarity score. These tasks are foundational to various real-
world applications such as question answering systems plagiarism detection, information retrieval
and duplicate content identification.
This project’s main goal is to design and assess a deep learning system for paraphrase detection
and semantic textual similarity. It will compare traditional neural architectures with modern
transformer-based models to understand their eﬀectiveness in capturing semantic relationships
between sentence pairs. Specifically, the project will investigate how contextualised word
representations enhance semantic comprehension compared to static embeddings and how fine-
tuning pretrained language models improves performance on downstream NLP tasks.
The proposed system will process pairs of sentences and generate dense vector representations
using deep neural models. Initial experiments will focus on baseline approaches like Siamese
neural networks with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) or Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) encoders.
These architectures independently encode each sentence and calculate similarity using distance
metrics like cosine or Manhattan distance. Building on these, the project will introduce
transformer-based architectures such as BERT or its variants. These architectures utilise self-
attention mechanisms to better model contextual dependencies. Fine-tuning these models on
labelled paraphrase and similarity datasets is anticipated to significantly enhance performance.
For training and evaluation, publicly available benchmark datasets like the Microsoft Research
Paraphrase Corpus (MRPC), Quora Question Pairs (QQP) and Semantic Textual Similarity
Benchmark (STS-B) will be utilised. Appropriate preprocessing steps including tokenisation,
sentence normalisation and handling out-of-vocabulary terms will be applied. Model performance
will be assessed using standard metrics like accuracy, F1-score, Pearson correlation and
Spearman rank correlation depending on the task.
The project’s goal is to compare deep learning architectures for semantic similarity tasks,
highlighting their strengths and weaknesses. It will also oﬀer insights into the trade-oﬀs between
computational complexity and model performance. Ultimately, this project aims to demonstrate
how deep learning models can eﬀectively capture semantic equivalence and similarity in natural
language, underscoring their significance in modern NLP systems.
